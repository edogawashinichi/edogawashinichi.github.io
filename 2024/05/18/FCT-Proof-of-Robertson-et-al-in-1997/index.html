FCT Proof of Robertson et al. in 1997

The Four Color Theorem (abbreviated as FCT)
has a long and notorious history.
So far its computer-based proof is not widely accepted.
This article tries to read the paper of Robertson intensively.

Basic knowledge on graph theory and FCT is assumed.
We’ll document concepts and theorems in the following pipeline.

Concept0 Near Circuit

A set of edges of a graph is a near-circuit,
provided it forms a circuit of the graph
by adding exactly one edge in the graph.
A circuit of a graph is a cycle of it with
no repeated vertices and edges.

Concept1 Sparse Edge Set

Note that any minimal counterexample of FCT must be triangulated
(each face is a triangle).

A set of edges of a minimal counterexample of FCT
is sparse, provided it takes at most one edge
from each triangle of the counterexample.

The complement of a sparse set is dense.

Concept2 Tri Coloring Modulo

Let $T$ be a minimal counterexample of FCT.
Let $X\subset E(T)$ be sparse (may be $\varnothing$).
Let $\overline X:=E(T)-X$ be the dense complement.
Let $\kappa:\overline X\longrightarrow\lbrace-1,0,1\rbrace$ be a $3$-edge-coloring of $T$
restricted to $\overline X$ (may be improper).
Then $\kappa$ is a tri-coloring of $G$ modulo $X$, provided
for any triangle of $T$ (with edges denoted by $e,f,g$),
(i) if $\lbrace e,f,g\rbrace\subset\overline X$,
$\ \ \ \ $then $\lbrace\kappa(e),\kappa(f),\kappa(g)\rbrace=\lbrace-1,0,1\rbrace$. (proper colored triangle)
(ii) otherwise say $\lbrace e,f\rbrace\subset\overline X$,
$\ \ \ \ \ \ $then $\kappa(e)=\kappa(f)$. (improper colored triangle)

Note that a tri-coloring of $T$ modulo $\varnothing$ is a proper $3$-edge-coloring of $T$.

Concept3 Quotient Graph

Let $H$ be a subgraph of graph $G$.
Let $H_1,H_2,\cdots,H_k$ be all the components of $H$.
Then the graph $Q$ is the quotient graph of $G$ modulo $H$, provided
$Q$ is obtained from $G$ by merging/contracting each component of $H$ to a single vertex,
and we denote this by $Q:=G/H:=G/H_1/H_2/\cdots/H_k$.

Note that quotient graphs preserve planarity.

Concept4 Quotient Extension Coloring

Let $H$ be a subgraph of graph $G$.
Let $H_1,H_2,\cdots,H_k$ be all the components of $H$.
Let $Q$ be the quotient graph of $G$ modulo $H$,
particularly letting $V(Q):=\lbrace H_1,H_2,\cdots,H_k\rbrace$
and $E(Q):=\lbrace (v_i,v_j)\in E(G):\ v_i\in V(H_i),\ v_j\in V(H_j),\ (v_i,v_j)\notin E(H)\rbrace$.
Supposing that $Q$ is loopless, let $\phi:V(Q)\longrightarrow\mathbb{Z}$ be a vertex-coloring of $Q$.
Then $\phi:V(G)\longrightarrow\mathbb{Z}$ is a quotient extension coloring from $Q$ to $G$,
provided $\phi(v):=\phi(H_i)$ for any $v\in V(H_i)$.

Note that the quotient extension coloring may be improper.

Concept5 Tait Coloring Transformation

Let $\phi:V(G)\longrightarrow\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_2$ be a proper $4$-vertex-coloring of graph $G$.
Then $\phi:E(G)\longrightarrow\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_2$,
defined by $\phi(e):=\phi(u)+\phi(v)$ for any $e=(u,v)\in E(G)$,
is a proper $3$-edge-coloring of $G$
(Noting that $\phi(e)=(0,0)$ implies $\phi(u)=\phi(v)$ which is impossible).
This is a Tait Coloring Transformation of $\phi$ from vertices to edges.

Proposition6 Nearcircuit Sparse Tricoloring

$\underline{\textbf{Proposition6}}$
Let $T$ be a minimal counterexample of FCT.
Let $X\subset E(T)$ be non-empty, sparse, and not a near-circuit.
Then $T$ admits a tri-coloring modulo $X$.

PROOF:
Firstly, we construct the quotient graph $S:=T/H$,
where the subgraph $H:=(V(T), X)$. Then we have
(i) $S$ is loopless(allow vertex coloring), because $X$ is not a near-circuit;
(ii) $S$ is a smaller(because non-empty) planar graph than $T$;
Therefore $S$ admits a proper $4$-vertex-coloring $\phi$,
which implies a quotient extension coloring of $T$.
Secondly, by Tait coloring transformation of $\phi$ from $V(T)$ to $E(T)$,
we obtain a $3$-edge-coloring of $T$.
Then restricting $\phi$ from $E(T)$ to $\overline X:=E(T)-X$ we obtain $\kappa:=\phi_{\overline X}$.
As can be verified, $\kappa$ is a tri-coloring modulo $X$.
At last, let $\lbrace(0,1),(1,1),(1,0)\rbrace\longleftrightarrow\lbrace-1,0,1\rbrace$,
to make the binary colors the same as colors in the computer program.
$\square$
